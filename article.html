Article on Gender Equality

From the Book of Genesis, Chapter One, Verse Twenty-Seven: God created man, and the basis was His own image. This shows that you, I, or all of us, regardless of gender, have equal dignity and value in the eyes of God. If all people, regardless of gender, were created in God's image, how can we truly demonstrate equality in our society? Gender equality is one of the seventeen sustainable development goals established by the United Nations. Its aim is to end all forms of discrimination against women and children and ensure they have equal rights and opportunities in all aspects of life. Why is gender equality important? It is vital because it upholds a just and progressive society. When men and women have equal rights, a country’s economy, education, and peace have a higher chance of thriving. But what if this modern and humane world we are striving for still hides outdated ideologies and toxic beliefs on its fringes? Nine years later, challenges in achieving equal treatment between genders remain, with women experiencing these struggles the most. First, workplace discrimination. The wage gap persists, where women earn less than men even when doing the same job with equal responsibilities. Imagine a woman, intelligent, educated, and experienced, applying for a high position. Yet a man with the same qualifications is often given priority.

What’s more painful is that this is not just one woman’s story, it is the reality of many. A silent yet glaringly obvious system says: "You’re just a woman." We cannot allow this. In today’s time, stricter anti discrimination laws should be enforced to eliminate workplace discrimination and ensure equal pay. Second, violence and abuse. When a woman becomes a victim of violence, whether physical, emotional, or sexual, people ask, “What was she wearing?” or “Why was she out late at night?” The rotten idea that the victim is to blame for her trauma still prevails. Shouldn’t the better question be why people commit violence and why perpetrators remain free? It would be a great help to establish more women and child protection units and counseling centers as safe havens for victims. But it’s not just women who suffer. Men can also be victims of abuse, particularly physical or emotional. In today’s society, men are often expected to hide vulnerability. A man admitting he is hurt is sometimes seen as a loss of honor. Starting now, we should educate both the youth and society as a whole, fostering awareness to recognize individuals based on their unique traits, not their gender. Third, discrimination in education. In Afghanistan, young people, especially girls, are denied the right to education. Here in the Philippines, regardless of gender, we can freely pursue education. But in other parts of the world, girls are locked behind bars, kept away from schools.

In a time when education is the key to rising above life’s difficulties, how can we open the lock when the door to opportunity is being withheld and buried? Gender equality issues are not just women’s problems; they are problems for all of us. By accepting the current situation, we are also accepting an unequal future. Let us start with ourselves today. Let us discard outdated notions about gender rooted in the past. Discrimination has been part of our history, but it is something we should no longer revisit.
